There are certain questions that I find myself asking on a fairly regular basis. What should I do in the future? What should I do right now? How should what I need do in the future affect what I should do now? What do I even want to do? Is what I want to do truly meaningful? What does it mean for something to be meaningful? Though I am lucky enough to currently be in a position of relative comfort, I can imagine going through horrible events that also make me question what the meaning of everything is. These questions that tend to linger on one's mind often boil down to questions about *meaning*.

To me, it seems that more likely than not, there is no such thing as objective meaning. The immediate response to this realization can be what Viktor Frankl termed the *existential vacuum*: an inner emptiness and a lack of awareness of any meaning worth living for. Frankl defines the existential vacuum as an experience of *inner emptiness* which is caused by a *lack of awareness of a meaning worth living for*. The existential vacuum does not necessarily have a single, intelligible cause. It can build slowly over time due to a variety of traumatic experiences or hit you all at once for seemingly no reason. Keeping oneself busy can seem like a solution but it inevitably becomes hard to shake the feeling that one is living in denial of the reality of meaninglessness. 

Frankl, as a psychologist, offers a potent antidote to this despair through *Logotherapy*. He argues that meaning is not something we invent out of thin air, but something we _discover_ in the world, even in the most dire of circumstances. Frankl, having experienced unimaginable horrors in the Holocaust, does not care whether meaning is part of the metaphysical fabric of the universe. He does not seek to understand the abstract concept of meaning. Perhaps he believes that such an effort is pointless. He knows the horrors that life has to offer, and gives suggestions to withstand them.

Albert Camus has a different approach compared to Frankl. Camus, having a stronger philosophical tendancy, cannot bring himself to abandon reason. He believes that the desire to understand is central to our being. And it is the tension between this desire and meaninglessness that creates what Camus calls the *absurd*. To Camus, this tension is not something that can or even should be relieved. Being dishonest is the greatest sin. He actively renounces any attempt to cope with the absurd. He believes that the only right way to live is to live directly staring into the abyss.
