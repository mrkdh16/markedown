The following is a list of all theorems, lemmas, and corollaries found in the sources, ordered sequentially by section number (1 through 31, plus the beginning of 32):

### Section 2: Binary Operations

- **2.13 Theorem (Associativity of Composition):** Composition of functions mapping $S$ into $S$ is associative.

### Section 3: Isomorphic Binary Structures

- **3.13 Theorem (Uniqueness of Identity Element):** A binary structure $(S, *)$ has at most one identity element.
- **3.14 Theorem:** If $(S, _)$ has an identity element $e$ for $_$, if $\phi: S \to S'$ is an isomorphism of $(S, _)$ with $(S', _')$, then $\phi(e)$ is an identity element for $_'$, and if $a \in S$ has an inverse $a'$ for $a$ under $_$, then $\phi(a')$ is an inverse for $\phi(a)$ under $*'$.

### Section 4: Groups

- **4.17 Theorem:** In a group $G$ with binary operation $*$, there is only one element $e$ in $G$ such that $e * x = x * e = x$ for all $x \in G$. For each $a \in G$, there is only one element $a'$ in $G$ such that $a * a' = a' * a = e$.
- **4.18 Corollary:** If $G$ is a group, for all $a, b \in G$, we have $(a * b)' = b' * a'$.

### Section 5: Subgroups

- **5.14 Theorem:** A subset $H$ of a group $G$ is a subgroup of $G$ if and only if: 1. $H$ is closed under the binary operation of $G$. 2. The identity element $e$ of $G$ is in $H$. 3. For all $a \in H$, it is true that $a^{-1} \in H$.
- **5.17 Theorem:** Let $G$ be a group and let $a \in G$. The subgroup ${a^n | n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ of $G$ is the **cyclic subgroup** of $G$ generated by $a$, denoted by $\langle a \rangle$.

### Section 6: Cyclic Groups

- **6.1 Theorem:** Every cyclic group is abelian.
- **6.6 Theorem:** A subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.
- **6.7 Corollary:** The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$ under addition are precisely the groups $n\mathbb{Z}$ under addition for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.
- **6.10 Theorem:** Let $G$ be a cyclic group with generator $a$. If the order of $G$ is infinite, then $G$ is isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$. If $G$ has finite order $n$, then $G$ is isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}_n, +_n)$.
- **6.14 Theorem:** Let $G$ be a cyclic group with $n$ elements and generated by $a$. Let $b \in G$ and let $b = a^s$. Then $b$ generates a cyclic subgroup $H$ of $G$ containing $n/d$ elements, where $d$ is the greatest common divisor of $n$ and $s$.
- **6.16 Corollary:** If $a$ is a generator of a finite cyclic group $G$ of order $n$, then the other generators of $G$ are the elements of the form $a^r$, where $r$ is relatively prime to $n$.

### Section 7: Generating Sets and Cayley Digraphs

- **7.6 Theorem:** If $G$ is a group and $a_i \in G$ for $i \in I$, then the subgroup $H$ of $G$ generated by ${a_i | i \in I}$ has as elements precisely those elements of $G$ that are finite products of integral powers of the $a_i$, where powers of a fixed $a_i$ may occur several times in the product.

### Section 8: Groups of Permutations

- **8.5 Theorem:** Let $A$ be a nonempty set, and let $S_A$ be the collection of all permutations of $A$. Then $S_A$ is a group under permutation multiplication.
- **8.15 Lemma:** Let $G$ and $G'$ be groups and let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a one-to-one function such that $\phi(xy) = \phi(x)\phi(y)$ for all $x, y \in G$. Then $\phi[G]$ is a subgroup of $G'$ and $\phi$ provides an isomorphism of $G$ with $\phi[G]$.
- **8.16 Theorem (Cayley's Theorem):** Every group is isomorphic to a group of permutations.

### Section 9: Orbits, Cycles, and the Alternating Groups

- **9.8 Theorem:** Every permutation $\sigma$ of a finite set is a product of disjoint cycles.
- **9.12 Corollary:** Any permutation of a finite set of at least two elements is a product of transpositions.
- **9.20 Theorem:** If $n \ge 2$, then the collection of all even permutations of ${1, 2, 3, \dots, n}$ forms a subgroup of order $n!/2$ of the symmetric group $S_n$.

### Section 10: Cosets and the Theorem of Lagrange

- **10.1 Theorem:** Let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. The relation $\sim_L$ defined on $G$ by $a \sim_L b$ if and only if $a^{-1}b \in H$ is an equivalence relation.
- **10.10 Theorem (Lagrange's Theorem):** If $G$ is a finite group and $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $|G|/|H|$ is the number of distinct left (and right) cosets of $H$ in $G$.
- **10.14 Theorem:** Suppose $H$ and $K$ are subgroups of a group $G$ such that $K \subseteq H \subseteq G$, and suppose $(H:K)$ and $(G:H)$ are both finite. Then $(G:K)$ is finite, and $(G:K) = (G:H)(H:K)$.

### Section 11: Direct Products and Finitely Generated Abelian Groups

- **11.2 Theorem:** Let $G_1, G_2, \dots, G_n$ be groups. The set $G_1 \times G_2 \times \dots \times G_n$ is a group under componentwise multiplication (the **direct product**).
- **11.5 Theorem:** The group $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ is cyclic and isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_{mn}$ if and only if $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime, i.e., $\text{gcd}(m, n) = 1$.
- **11.6 Corollary:** The group $\prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{Z}_{m_i}$ is cyclic and isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_{m_1 m_2 \dots m_n}$ if and only if the numbers $m_i$ for $i = 1, \dots, n$ are such that $\text{gcd}(m_i, m_j) = 1$ when $i \ne j$.
- **11.9 Theorem:** Let $\langle a_1 \rangle$ in $G_1, \dots, \langle a_n \rangle$ in $G_n$ be finite cyclic groups. If $a = (a_1, \dots, a_n)$ in $G_1 \times \dots \times G_n$ is of finite order, $r_i$ in $G_i$, then the order of $(a_1, \dots, a_n)$ in $\prod_{i=1}^n G_i$ is equal to the least common multiple of all the $r_i$.
- **11.12 Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Finitely Generated Abelian Groups):** Every finitely generated abelian group $G$ is isomorphic to a direct product of cyclic groups in the form $\mathbb{Z}_{p_1^{r_1}} \times \mathbb{Z}_{p_2^{r_2}} \times \dots \times \mathbb{Z}_{p_n^{r_n}} \times \mathbb{Z} \times \dots \times \mathbb{Z}$.
- **11.15 Theorem:** The finite indecomposable abelian groups are exactly the cyclic groups with order a power of a prime.
- **11.16 Theorem:** If $m$ divides the order of a finite abelian group $G$, then $G$ has a subgroup of order $m$.
- **11.17 Theorem:** If $m$ is a square free integer, meaning $m$ is not divisible by the square of any prime, then every abelian group of order $m$ is cyclic.

### Section 13: Homomorphisms

- **13.12 Theorem:** Let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a group homomorphism. 1. If $e$ is the identity element in $G$, then $\phi(e) = e'$ in $G'$. 2. If $a \in G$, then $\phi(a^{-1}) = \phi(a)^{-1}$. 3. If $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $\phi[H]$ is a subgroup of $G'$. 4. If $K'$ is a subgroup of $G'$, then $\phi^{-1}[K']$ is a subgroup of $G$.
- **13.15 Theorem:** Let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a group homomorphism, and let $H = \text{Ker}(\phi)$. Let $a \in G$. Then the set $\phi^{-1}[{\phi(a)}] = aH = Ha$. Consequently, the two partitions of $G$ into left cosets and into right cosets of $H$ are the same.
- **13.18 Corollary:** A group homomorphism $\phi: G \to G'$ is a one-to-one map if and only if $\text{Ker}(\phi) = {e}$.
- **13.20 Corollary:** If $\phi: G \to G'$ is a group homomorphism, then $\text{Ker}(\phi)$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

### Section 14: Factor Groups

- **14.1 Theorem:** Let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a group homomorphism with kernel $H$. Then the cosets of $H$ form a **factor group** $G/H$, where $(aH)(bH) = (ab)H$. The map $\mu: G/H \to \phi[G]$ defined by $\mu(aH) = \phi(a)$ is an isomorphism.
- **14.4 Theorem:** Let $H$ be a subgroup of a group $G$. Left coset multiplication is well defined by the equation $(aH)(bH) = (ab)H$ if and only if $H$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.
- **14.5 Corollary:** Let $H$ be a normal subgroup of $G$. Then the cosets of $H$ form a group $G/H$ under the binary operation $(aH)(bH) = (ab)H$.
- **14.9 Theorem (The Fundamental Homomorphism Theorem):** Let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a group homomorphism with kernel $H$. Then $\phi[G]$ is a group, and $\mu: G/H \to \phi[G]$ given by $\mu(gH) = \phi(g)$ is an isomorphism.
- **14.13 Theorem:** The following are three equivalent conditions for a subgroup $H$ of a group $G$ to be a normal subgroup of $G$: 1. $g h g^{-1} \in H$ for all $g \in G$ and $h \in H$. 2. $g H = H g$ for all $g \in G$. 3. $g H g^{-1} = H$ for all $g \in G$.

### Section 15: Factor-Group Computations and Simple Groups

- **15.8 Theorem:** Let $G = H \times K$ be the direct product of groups $H$ and $K$. Then $\bar{H} = {(h, e) | h \in H}$ is a normal subgroup of $G$. Also $G/\bar{H}$ is isomorphic to $K$ in a natural way.
- **15.9 Theorem:** A factor group of a cyclic group is cyclic.
- **15.15 Theorem:** The alternating group $A_n$ is simple for $n \ge 5$.
- **15.16 Theorem:** Let $\phi: G \to G'$ be a group homomorphism. If $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, then $\phi[N]$ is a normal subgroup of $\phi[G]$. Also, if $N'$ is a normal subgroup of $G'$, then $\phi^{-1}[N']$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

### Section 16: Group Action on a Set

- **16.3 Theorem:** Let $X$ be a $G$-set. For each $g \in G$, the map $\sigma_g: X \to X$ defined by $\sigma_g(x) = g x$ for $x \in X$ is a permutation of $X$. Also, the map $\phi: G \to S_X$ defined by $\phi(g) = \sigma_g$ is a homomorphism.
- **16.14 Theorem:** Let $X$ be a $G$-set, and let $x \in X$. Then $|Gx|$ is the size of the orbit of $x$, and if $|G|$ is finite, then $|Gx|$ is a divisor of $|G|$.
- **16.14 Theorem (continuation/equivalence relation):** $x \sim y$ if and only if there exists $g \in G$ such that $gx = y$. Then $\sim$ is an equivalence relation on $X$.

### Section 18: Rings and Fields

- **18.8 Theorem:** If $R$ is a ring with additive identity $0$, then for any $a, b \in R$ we have: 1. $0a = a0 = 0$. 2. $a(-b) = (-a)b = -(ab)$. 3. $(-a)(-b) = ab$.

### Section 19: Integral Domains

- **19.5 Theorem:** The cancellation laws hold in a ring $R$ if and only if $R$ has no divisors of zero.
- **19.9 Theorem:** Every field $F$ is an integral domain.
- **19.15 Theorem:** Let $R$ be a ring with unity. If $n \cdot 1 = 0$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, then $R$ has characteristic $0$. If $n \cdot 1 = 0$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, then the smallest such integer $n$ is the characteristic of $R$.

### Section 20: Fermat's and Euler's Theorems

- **20.1 Theorem (Little Theorem of Fermat):** If $a \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $p$ is a prime not dividing $a$, then $p$ divides $a^{p-1} - 1$, that is, $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 (\text{mod } p)$.
- **20.2 Corollary:** If $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, then $a^p \equiv a (\text{mod } p)$ for any prime $p$.
- **20.6 Theorem (Euler's Theorem):** If $a$ is an integer relatively prime to $n$, then $a^{\phi(n)} - 1$ is divisible by $n$, that is, $a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 (\text{mod } n)$.
- **20.8 Theorem (Euler's Theorem):** If $a$ is an integer relatively prime to $n$, then $a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 (\text{mod } n)$.
- **20.10 Theorem:** Let $m$ be a positive integer and let $a \in \mathbb{Z}_m$. The equation $a x = b$ has a unique solution in $\mathbb{Z}_m$ if and only if $a$ is a unit in $\mathbb{Z}_m$.
- **20.11 Corollary:** If $a$ and $m$ are relatively prime integers, then for any integer $b$, the congruence $a x \equiv b (\text{mod } m)$ has solutions, and all solutions are precisely one residue class modulo $m$.
- **20.12 Theorem:** Let $m$ be a positive integer and let $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_m$. Let $d$ be the gcd of $a$ and $m$. The equation $a x = b$ has a solution in $\mathbb{Z}_m$ if and only if $d$ divides $b$. When $d$ divides $b$, the equation has exactly $d$ solutions in $\mathbb{Z}_m$.
- **20.13 Corollary:** Let $d$ be the gcd of positive integers $a$ and $m$. The congruence $a x \equiv b (\text{mod } m)$ has a solution if and only if $d$ divides $b$. When this is the case, the solutions are the integers in exactly $d$ distinct residue classes modulo $m$.

### Section 21: The Field of Quotients of an Integral Domain

- **21.2 Lemma:** The relation $\sim$ between elements of the set $S$ (ordered pairs $(a, b)$ with $b \ne 0$) defined by $(a, b) \sim (c, d)$ if and only if $ad = bc$ is an equivalence relation.
- **21.3 Lemma:** Addition and multiplication, defined as $[(a, b)] + [(c, d)] = [(ad + bc, bd)]$ and $[(a, b)][(c, d)] = [(ac, bd)]$, give well-defined operations on addition and multiplication on $F$ (the set of equivalence classes).
- **21.4 Lemma:** The map $i: D \to F$ given by $i(a) = [(a, 1)]$ is an isomorphism of $D$ with a subring of $F$.
- **21.5 Theorem:** Any integral domain $D$ can be enlarged to (or embedded in) a field $F$ such that every element of $F$ can be expressed as a quotient of two elements of $D$. (Such a field $F$ is a **field of quotients** of $D$).
- **21.6 Theorem (Uniqueness):** Let $F$ be a field of quotients of $D$ and let $L$ be any field containing $D$. Then there exists a map $\psi: F \to L$ that gives an isomorphism of $F$ with a subfield of $L$ such that $\psi(a) = a$ for $a \in D$.
- **21.8 Corollary:** Every integral domain $L$ containing an integral domain $D$ contains a field of quotients of $D$.
- **21.9 Corollary:** Any two fields of quotients of an integral domain $D$ are isomorphic.

### Section 22: Rings of Polynomials

- **22.2 Theorem:** The set $R[x]$ of all polynomials in an indeterminate $x$ with coefficients in a ring $R$ is a ring under polynomial addition and multiplication. If $R$ is commutative, then so is $R[x]$, and if $R$ has unity $1 \ne 0$, then $1$ is also unity for $R[x]$.
- **22.4 Theorem (The Evaluation Homomorphism for Field Theory):** Let $F$ be a subfield of a field $E$. Let $\alpha$ be an element of $E$. The map $\phi_\alpha: F[x] \to E$ defined by $\phi_\alpha(a_0 + a_1 x + \dots + a_n x^n) = a_0 + a_1 \alpha + \dots + a_n \alpha^n$ is a homomorphism of $F[x]$ into $E$.
- **22.11 Theorem:** The polynomial $x^2 - 2$ has no zeros in the rational numbers. Thus $\sqrt{2}$ is not a rational number.

### Section 23: Factorization of Polynomials over a Field

- **23.1 Theorem (Division Algorithm for $F[x]$):** Let $f(x) \in F[x]$ and $g(x) \in F[x]$ be two polynomials in a field $F$, with $g(x) \ne 0$. There are unique polynomials $q(x)$ and $r(x)$ in $F[x]$ such that $f(x) = g(x)q(x) + r(x)$, where either $r(x) = 0$ or the degree of $r(x)$ is less than the degree of $g(x)$.
- **23.3 Corollary (Factor Theorem):** An element $a \in F$ is a zero of $f(x) \in F[x]$ if and only if $x - a$ is a factor of $f(x)$ in $F[x]$.
- **23.5 Corollary:** A nonzero polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ of degree $n$ can have at most $n$ zeros in a field $F$.
- **23.6 Corollary:** If $G$ is a finite subgroup of the multiplicative group $(F^\times, \cdot)$ of a field $F$, then $G$ is cyclic. In particular, the multiplicative group of all nonzero elements of a finite field is cyclic.
- **23.10 Theorem:** Let $F$ be a field and let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be of degree 2 or 3. Then $f(x)$ is reducible over $F$ if and only if it has a zero in $F$.
- **23.11 Theorem:** If $f(x) \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$, then $f(x)$ factors into a product of two polynomials of lower degrees $r$ and $s$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ if and only if it has such a factorization with polynomials of the same degrees $r$ and $s$ in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$.
- **23.12 Corollary:** If $f(x) = x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \dots + a_0$ is in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ with $a_0 \ne 0$, and if $f(x)$ has a zero in $\mathbb{Q}$, then it has a zero $m$ in $\mathbb{Z}$, and $m$ must divide $a_0$.
- **23.15 Theorem (Eisenstein Criterion):** Let $p \in \mathbb{Z}$ be a prime. Suppose that $f(x) = a_n x^n + \dots + a_0$ is in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, and $a_n \not\equiv 0 (\text{mod } p)$, but $a_i \equiv 0 (\text{mod } p)$ for all $i < n$, with $a_0 \not\equiv 0 (\text{mod } p^2)$. Then $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$.
- **23.17 Corollary:** The polynomial $\Phi_p(x) = \frac{x^p - 1}{x-1} = x^{p-1} + x^{p-2} + \dots + x + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ for any prime $p$.
- **23.18 Theorem:** Let $p(x) \in F[x]$ be an irreducible polynomial. If $p(x)$ divides $r(x)s(x)$ for $r(x), s(x) \in F[x]$, then either $p(x)$ divides $r(x)$ or $p(x)$ divides $s(x)$.
- **23.19 Corollary:** If $p(x)$ is irreducible in $F[x]$ and $p(x)$ divides the product $r_1(x) \dots r_n(x)$ for $r_i(x) \in F[x]$, then $p(x)$ divides $r_i(x)$ for at least one $i$.
- **23.20 Theorem:** If $F$ is a field, then every nonconstant polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ can be factored in $F[x]$ into a product of irreducible polynomials, the irreducible polynomials being unique except for order and for unit factors in $F$.

### Section 26: Homomorphisms and Factor Rings

- **26.3 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 13.12):** Properties of ring homomorphisms relating to identity, additive inverse, subrings, and inverse images of subrings.
- **26.5 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 13.15):** Let $\phi: R \to R'$ be a ring homomorphism with kernel $H$. Then $\phi^{-1}[{\phi(a)}] = a + H = H + a$, where $a + H$ is the coset containing $a$ of the commutative additive group $(H, +)$.
- **26.6 Corollary (Analogue of Corollary 13.18):** A ring homomorphism $\phi: R \to R'$ is a one-to-one map if and only if $\text{Ker}(\phi) = {0}$.
- **26.7 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 14.1):** Let $\phi: R \to R'$ be a ring homomorphism with kernel $H$. The additive cosets of $H$ form a ring $R/H$, and the map $\mu: R/H \to \phi[R]$ defined by $\mu(a+H) = \phi(a)$ is an isomorphism.
- **26.9 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 14.4):** Let $H$ be a subring of $R$. Multiplication of additive cosets of $H$ is well defined by $(a+H)(b+H) = (ab) + H$ if and only if $H$ is an ideal of $R$.
- **26.14 Corollary (Analogue of Corollary 14.5):** Let $N$ be an ideal of a ring $R$. Then the additive cosets of $N$ form a ring $R/N$ (the **factor ring** or **quotient ring**).
- **26.16 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 14.9):** Let $N$ be an ideal of a ring $R$. Then $\gamma: R \to R/N$ given by $\gamma(x) = x + N$ is a ring homomorphism with kernel $N$.
- **26.17 Theorem (Fundamental Homomorphism Theorem: Analogue of Theorem 14.11):** Let $\phi: R \to R'$ be a ring homomorphism with kernel $N$. Then $\phi[R]$ is a ring, and $\mu: R/N \to \phi[R]$ given by $\mu(x+N) = \phi(x)$ is an isomorphism.

### Section 27: Prime and Maximal Ideals

- **27.5 Theorem:** Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity and $N$ an ideal of $R$. Then $R/N$ is an integral domain if and only if $N$ is a **prime ideal**.
- **27.9 Theorem (Analogue of Theorem 15.18):** Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity. Then $M$ is a maximal ideal of $R$ if and only if $R/M$ is a field.
- **27.11 Corollary:** A commutative ring with unity is a field if and only if it has no proper nontrivial ideals.
- **27.15 Theorem:** Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity, and let $N \ne R$ be an ideal in $R$. Then $R/N$ is an integral domain if and only if $N$ is a prime ideal in $R$.
- **27.16 Corollary:** Every maximal ideal in a commutative ring with unity is a prime ideal.
- **27.17 Theorem:** If $R$ is a ring with unity $1$, then the map $\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to R$ given by $\phi(n) = n \cdot 1$ is a homomorphism.
- **27.18 Corollary:** If $R$ is a ring with unity and $\text{char}(R) = n > 1$, then $R$ contains a subring isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_n$. If $\text{char}(R) = 0$, then $R$ contains a subring isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}$.
- **27.19 Theorem:** A field $F$ is either of prime characteristic $p$ and contains a subfield isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_p$, or of characteristic $0$ and contains a subfield isomorphic to $\mathbb{Q}$.
- **27.24 Theorem:** If $F$ is a field, every ideal in $F[x]$ is principal.
- **27.25 Theorem:** An ideal $\langle p(x) \rangle \ne {0}$ of $F[x]$ is maximal if and only if $p(x)$ is irreducible over $F$.
- **27.27 Theorem:** Let $p(x) \in F[x]$ be an irreducible polynomial. If $p(x)$ divides $r(x)s(x)$ for $r(x), s(x) \in F[x]$, then either $p(x)$ divides $r(x)$ or $p(x)$ divides $s(x)$.

### Section 29: Introduction to Extension Fields

- **29.3 Theorem (Kronecker's Theorem, Basic Goal):** Let $F$ be a field and let $f(x)$ be a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$. Then there exists an extension field $E$ of $F$ and an $\alpha \in E$ such that $f(\alpha) = 0$.
- **29.12 Theorem:** Let $E$ be an extension field of a field $F$, and let $\phi_\alpha: F[x] \to E$ be the evaluation homomorphism for $\alpha \in E$ over $F$. Then $\alpha$ is transcendental over $F$ if and only if $\phi_\alpha$ is a one-to-one map from $F[x]$ to $\phi_\alpha[F[x]]$.
- **29.13 Theorem:** Let $E$ be an extension field of $F$, and let $\alpha \in E$, where $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$. Then there is a unique irreducible monic polynomial $p(x)$ in $F[x]$ that has $\alpha$ as a zero.
- **29.18 Theorem:** Let $E$ be a simple extension field of a field $F$, and let $\alpha$ be algebraic over $F$. If $\deg(\text{irr}(\alpha, F)) = n$, then every element $\beta \in F(\alpha)$ can be uniquely expressed in the form $\beta = b_0 + b_1 \alpha + \dots + b_{n-1} \alpha^{n-1}$, where the $b_i$ are in $F$.

### Section 30: Vector Spaces

- **30.5 Theorem:** If $V$ is a vector space over $F$, then $0\alpha = 0$, $a0 = 0$, and $(-a)\alpha = a(-\alpha) = -(a\alpha)$ for all $a \in F$ and $\alpha \in V$.
- **30.17 Theorem:** Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $F$. Let $S = {\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n}$ be a subset of $V$. Then every vector $\beta$ in the span $\text{Sp}(S)$ can be uniquely expressed as a linear combination of the $\alpha_i$ if and only if $S$ is linearly independent.
- **30.18 Corollary:** A finite-dimensional vector space has a finite basis.
- **30.19 Theorem:** Let $S = {\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n}$ be a finite set of linearly independent vectors of a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ over a field $F$. Then $S$ can be enlarged to a basis for $V$ over $F$. Furthermore, if $B = {\beta_1, \dots, \beta_m}$ is any basis for $V$ over $F$, then $n \le m$.
- **30.20 Corollary:** Any two bases of a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ over $F$ have the same number of elements.
- **30.23 Theorem:** Let $E$ be an extension field of $F$, and let $\alpha \in E$ be algebraic over $F$. If $\deg(\text{irr}(\alpha, F)) = n$, then $F(\alpha)$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space over $F$ with basis ${1, \alpha, \dots, \alpha^{n-1}}$. Furthermore, every element $\beta$ of $F(\alpha)$ is algebraic over $F$ with $\deg(\beta, F)$ dividing $\deg(\alpha, F)$.

### Section 31: Algebraic Extensions

- **31.3 Theorem:** A finite extension field $E$ of a field $F$ is an algebraic extension of $F$.
- **31.4 Theorem:** If $E$ is a finite extension field of a field $F$, and $K$ is a finite extension field of $E$, then $K$ is a finite extension of $F$, and $[K:F] = [K:E][E:F]$.
- **31.11 Theorem:** Let $E$ be an algebraic extension of a field $F$. If $\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n \in E$, then $F(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n)$ is a finite extension of $F$.
- **31.12 Theorem:** Let $E$ be an extension field of $F$. Then $\bar{F}_E = {a \in E | a \text{ is algebraic over } F}$ is a subfield of $E$, the **algebraic closure** of $F$ in $E$.
- **31.13 Corollary:** The set of all algebraic numbers forms a field.
- **31.15 Theorem:** A field $F$ is algebraically closed if and only if every nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$ factors into linear factors.
- **31.16 Corollary:** An algebraically closed field has no proper algebraic extensions.
- **31.17 Theorem:** Every field $F$ has an **algebraic closure** $\bar{F}$, which is an algebraic extension $\bar{F}$ that is algebraically closed.
- **31.18 Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra):** The field $\mathbb{C}$ of complex numbers is an algebraically closed field.
- **31.21 Zorn's Lemma:** If $S$ is a partially ordered set such that every chain in $S$ has an upper bound in $S$, then $S$ has at least one maximal element.
- **31.22 Restated Theorem 31.17:** Every field $F$ has an algebraic closure.
