Throughout my (admittedly short so far) life I've had a number of different "dreams." I've dreamt of harnessing my creativity to design and erect buildings or create media. I've dreamt of unleashing my competitive nature to professionally play sports. I've dreamt of simply not having to enter the rat race. For one reason or another, I realized that these dreams were not meant to be. In hindsight, I recognize that these realizations were not entirely *my* choices. The people around me quietly steered me toward more intellectual work. My exposure to the bleak realities of competition at a young age developed a guttural aversion toward anything I view as hyper-competitive. The shattering of my past dreams made me realize that if I don't take control, I cannot have agency. I cannot do much about the externalities that have shaped me into who I am today. And I don't doubt that a myriad of externalities will shape who I will become in the future. What I could do, however, was choose to be intentional about who I am and deliberate about who I want to become. This is perhaps a cliche conclusion to come to. And again, likely developed under considerable western societal influence. Nonetheless, it's the conclusion I came to and I consider it mine.

>*For what it’s worth... it’s never too late, or in my case too early, to be whoever you want to be. There’s no time limit. Start whenever you want. You can change or stay the same. There are no rules to this thing. We can make the best or the worst of it. I hope you make the best of it.*
>
>-F. Scott Fitzgerald

As I entered college, I pledged to find exactly what it is that I want to do and hold on to it as tight as I could. I first sought to explore as much as I could. I developed a passion for math that only seemed to grow the deeper I went. Having always had a vague interest in computer science, I took some time to actually learn it. I tried my hand at physics and dipped my toes in philosophy. My college experience so far has been one long revelation on who I am, what ignites a fire within me, and who I want to be. And I think I now have a decent answer to all three of these questions.

As G.H. Hardy justified his lifelong pursuit of abstract mathematics seemingly far beyond the realm of ordinary human affairs through his essay, *A Mathematician's Apology*, I intend to outline a justification for my intellectual pursuit: to understand the mechanics behind deep neural networks. While I don't necessarily believe that everyone need justify their short-term, long-term, or even life-long pursuits, I am by nature a philosophically motivated person. Thus, I find it of paramount importance to understand and argue for why I want to do what I want to do.

>*I shall ask, then, why is it really worth while to make a serious study of mathematics? What is the proper justification of a mathematician’s life? And my answers will be, for the most part, such as are expected from a mathematician: I think that it is worth while, that there is ample justification.*
>
>G.H. Hardy

Importantly, I am invested in *understanding* learning mechanics. There is not much practical or pragmatic motivation for this (although I'd be lying if I said there was none). I believe to understand is a virtue in its own right. 

Foundational first-principles research on neural networks is at the intersection of many of my interests. 

  

Technical.

- Mathematical structure. Neural nets are masters of capturing statistical structure. (Manifold hypothesis, equivariant neural nets)
- The notion of information mathematically formalized. (Information theory, signal processing)
- Simple local interactions creating complex emergent phenomena. This idea is philosophically appealing to me and is at the heart of neural network research. (Statistical mechanics applied to NNs)
- High dimensionality in all its mystery, unintuitiveness, and elegance is of much interest to me. (Kernel methods, Gaussian processes)
- The role of randomness in creating order, interpretability, and predictive power. This too has a philosophical appeal to me. Randomness is in fact never truly chaotic and order never fails to emerge. (Random matrix theory applied to NNs)

Non-technical.

- The general aura of mystery surrounding neural network research is also an attractive factor.
- The idea of non-human intelligence is interesting. The idea that there is non-human intelligence that surpasses human intelligence in certain respects which we don’t necessarily understand is even more interesting.
- I see neural networks and deep learning as an example of man-made phenomena that is not well understood (as opposed to the natural phenomena not well understood which was the topic of much study in the 20th century). Developing an understanding for such phenomena seems intrinsically valuable.
- I would be lying if I said I didn’t take into consideration the future potential growth in this area and earning capability by throwing myself in this field.

  

Of course, it is impossible to know which desire/interest drives my motivation to pursue this field. In fact, it certainly changes constantly. Whatever the case, I don’t think I’ll regret pursuing this with everything I have. I believe I will have the flexibility to pivot if I really want to and I believe I have the potential for success if I choose to continue to pursue this.
