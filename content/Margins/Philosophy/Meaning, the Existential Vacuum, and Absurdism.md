There are certain questions that I find myself asking on a fairly regular basis. What should I do in the future? What should I do right now? How should what I need do in the future affect what I should do now? What do I even want to do? Is what I want to do truly meaningful? What does it mean for something to be meaningful? 

The *existential vacuum* is a term coined by psychologist Viktor Frankl. He defines the existential vacuum as an experience of *inner emptiness* which is caused by a *lack of awareness of a meaning worth living for*. The existential vacuum does not necessarily have a single, intelligible cause. It can build slowly over time due to a variety of traumatic experiences or hit you all at once for seemingly no reason. The experience need not persist, and it may be that it only happens on occasion. Some people can shrug it off as a one-off negative experience. Others may find it harder to shake the feeling that they are living in denial of the reality of meaninglessness.

To Frankl, the existential vacuum something that can be cured. As a psychologist, he offers an antidote through Logotherapy. He argues that meaning is not something we invent out of thin air, but something we _discover_ in the world, even in the most dire of circumstances. Frankl, having experienced unimaginable horrors in the Holocaust, does not care whether meaning is part of the metaphysical fabric of the universe. He does not seek to understand the abstract concept of meaning. Perhaps he believes that such an effort is pointless. He knows the horrors that life has to offer, and gives suggestions to withstand them.

To existential philosopher Camus, the recognition of the absurd meaningless reality of our world is not something that we should attempt to overcome. The existential vacuum is not to be cured. *The absurd* is more real than anything else and it commands a certain level of respect.
