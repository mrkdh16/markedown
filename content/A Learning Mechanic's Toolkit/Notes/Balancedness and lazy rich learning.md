### setup & definitions
Define a general loss function as such:
$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(f(x_{i};\theta)-y_{i})^{2}
$$The time derivative of this function can be expressed using a kernel, giving $\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}(x;\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Theta(x,x_{i};\theta)(y_{i}-f(x_{i};\theta))$, where the neural tangent kernel $\Theta(x,x^{\prime};\theta)$ is defined as the sum over parameters $p$ of $\eta_{\theta_{p}}\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta_{p}}|_{_{(x;\theta)}}\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta_{p}}|_{_{(x^{\prime};\theta)}}$. This relationship is derived from the chain rule where $\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}(x;\theta)=\sum_{p=1}^{P}\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta_{p}}(x;\theta)\frac{\partial\theta_{p}}{\partial t}$, and the continuous-time gradient descent parameter updates follow $\frac{\partial\theta_{p}}{\partial t}=-\eta_{\theta p}\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta_{p}}=-\eta_{\theta p}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(f(x_{i};\theta)-y_{i})\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta_{p}}(x_{i};\theta)$. Substituting the parameter updates back into the time derivative equation yields a nested summation that directly forms the kernel representation.

A specific "Toy Model" is then introduced where the function is strictly linear, defined as $f(x;\theta)=aw^{T}x$, with $a\in \mathbb{R}$ and $w\in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, making it a mapping from $\mathbb{R}^{d}$ to $\mathbb{R}$. For this model, the loss function evaluates to $L(a,w)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(aw^{T}x_{i}-y_{i})^{2}$. The individual elements of the kernel matrix, $K_{ij}=\Theta(x_{i},x_{j};\theta)$, are calculated as $\sum_{p=1}^{d}\eta_{w}a^{2}x_{i}^{(p)}x_{j}^{(p)}+\eta_{a}(w^{T}x_{i})(w^{T}x_{j})$, which mathematically simplifies to $\eta_{w}a^{2}x_{i}^{T}x_{j}+\eta_{a}x_{i}^{T}ww^{T}x_{j}$. In full matrix notation, the kernel is concisely represented as $K=X(\eta_{w}a^{2}I_{d}+\eta_{a}ww^{T})X^{T}$.

Focusing on the parameter space dynamics, the continuous-time gradient descent equations for the weights are given by $\dot{w}=-\eta_{w}\nabla_{w}L=-\eta_{w}\sum_{i}(a^{2}x_{i}x_{i}^{T}w-ay_{i}x_{i})$ and $\dot{a}=-\eta_{a}\nabla_{a}L=-\eta_{a}\sum_{i}(aw^{T}x_{i}x_{i}^{T}w-y_{i}w^{T}x_{i})$. Assuming the data is whitened such that $X^{T}X=I_{d}$, the system's normal equations take the form $X^{T}Xaw=X^{T}y$. The model features a specific conserved quantity throughout training, denoted as $\delta=\eta_{w}a^{2}-\eta_{a}||w||^{2}$, which arises directly because $\eta_{w}a\dot{a}=\eta_{a}w^{T}\dot{w}$. By defining the optimal least squares solution as $\beta^{*}:=X^{T}y$, the weight dynamics can be factorized and rewritten as $\dot{w}=\eta_{w}(a\beta^{*}-a^{2}w)$ and $\dot{a}=\eta_{a}(w^{T}\beta^{*}-a||w||^{2})$. These simplified equations imply that at any fixed points, $w$ completely aligns in the direction of $\beta^{*}$.

These learning dynamics can be monitored by defining two new invariant variables: $y:=a||w||$, which is invariant under rescaling symmetry, and $\phi:=\frac{w^{T}\beta^{*}}{||w||||\beta^{*}||}$, representing the cosine of the spherical angle between the weights and the target. Using the constraint that $\eta_{w}a^{2}+\eta_{a}||w||^{2}=\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4\eta_{a}\eta_{w}y^{2}}$, the time derivative $\dot{y}=\dot{a}w^{T}w+2a\frac{w^{T}\dot{w}}{||w||}$ evaluates precisely to $\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4\eta_{a}\eta_{w}y^{2}}(\phi||\beta^{*}||-y)$. The angular dynamics are governed by $\dot{\phi}$, which simplifies down to $\frac{2\eta_{a}\eta_{w}y||\beta^{*}||}{\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4\eta_{a}\eta_{w}y^{2}}-\delta}(1-\phi^{2})$. Without loss of generality, the learning rate constants $\eta_{a}$ and $\eta_{w}$ can be absorbed into the dynamics such that we can set $\eta_{a}\eta_{w}=1$, resulting in the final monitored equations $\dot{y}=\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4y^{2}}(\phi||\beta^{*}||-y)$ and $\dot{\phi}=\frac{2y||\beta^{*}||}{\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4y^{2}}-\delta}(1-\phi^{2})$.

In function space, the effective composite parameter is defined as $\beta:=aw$, and the objective of training is simply for $\beta$ to approach $\beta^{*}$. The continuous dynamics of this parameter are $\dot{\beta}=\dot{a}w+a\dot{w}=-(\eta_{w}a^{2}I_{d}+\eta_{a}ww^{T})X^{T}\rho$, where $\rho=X\beta-y$ defines the current residual. This evolution can be accurately interpreted as a preconditioned gradient flow where the preconditioner matrix $M$ depends continuously on time. Specifically, $M$ can be constructed algebraically as $M=\frac{\bar{K}+\delta}{2}I_{d}+\frac{\bar{K}-\delta}{2}\frac{\beta\beta^{T}}{||\beta||^{2}}$, where the scaling term is $\bar{K}=\sqrt{\delta^{2}+4\eta_{w}\eta_{a}||\beta||^{2}}$. Ultimately, the function space dynamics $\dot{\beta}=MX^{T}\rho$ depend entirely on $\beta$ itself and are strictly regulated by the conserved quantity $\delta$.

The behavior of these learning dynamics is heavily characterized by the chosen value of $\delta$, effectively splitting into upstream, balanced, and downstream regimes. In the upstream regime ($\delta \gg 0$), if initialization is small such that $\delta^{2} \gg ||\beta||^{2}$, the preconditioner behaves essentially as a constant matrix $M \approx \delta I_{d}$ similar to the NTK regime, meaning the function space dynamics simplify to $\dot{\beta} \approx \delta X^{T}\rho$. The corresponding parameter space dynamics under these conditions are roughly $\dot{y} \approx \delta(\phi||\beta^{*}||-y)$ and $\dot{\phi} \approx \frac{y||\beta^{*}||}{\delta}(1-\phi^{2})$. For the balanced regime ($\delta \approx 0$), the preconditioner initially starts near zero for small initializations and operates much like standard linear regression. Because $y \approx 0$ near initialization while $\phi \rightarrow 1$, the magnitude changes only become non-negligible when $y$ scales, causing the temporal dynamics to fully decouple into an initial alignment phase where $\phi \rightarrow \pm 1$, followed sequentially by a fitting phase where magnitude scales up. In this balanced state, the parameter space dynamics are well approximated by $\dot{y} \approx 2y(\phi||\beta^{*}||-y)$ and $\dot{\phi} \approx ||\beta^{*}||(1-\phi^{2})$. Finally, in the downstream regime where $\delta \ll 0$, the behavior changes drastically; if $\delta \rightarrow -\infty$, the preconditioner aligns purely with the outer product of $\beta$ such that $M \approx |\delta|\frac{\beta\beta^{T}}{||\beta||^{2}}$.

These distinct learning behaviors and their implicit biases are visually illustrated in Figure 4 of the notes, mapping the trajectories of $\beta=aw$ across different values of $\delta$ starting from the exact same initial point $\beta_{0}$. When the data covariance matrix $X^{T}X$ is perfectly whitened, the dynamics follow exact, solvable analytical expressions plotted as black dashed lines. Upstream initializations move along a direct trajectory resembling standard gradient flow on $\beta$, while downstream initializations dramatically hook inward toward the origin before sweeping around circularly to the target $\beta^{*}$. Balanced initializations reliably trace an intermediate curved path directly between these two extremes. Conversely, when $X^{T}X$ is low-rank, the exact trajectories can only be explicitly predicted at the infinite limits of $\delta = \pm\infty$, though exact solutions, marked by black dots, can still be mapped if the interpolating manifold is strictly one-dimensional.
