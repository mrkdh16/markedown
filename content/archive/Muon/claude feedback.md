## The high-level narrative arc
Your "Plan of Attack" sets up five claims, and the body roughly follows them, but there's a structural mismatch that hurts the flow. The Plan of Attack is organized around _concepts_ (phases, conservation, alignment, decoupling, schedule), but the body is organized around _settings_ (scalar/scalar, scalar/vector, overparameterized, the schedule). These two organizations are both reasonable, but you switch between them without telling the reader you're doing so, so the mapping isn't obvious.

Concretely: a reader finishes the Plan of Attack with five labeled claims in their head, then hits Section 2 ("The Muon Phases of Training"), which is claim 1, then Section 3 ("Gaining Intuition from Simple Settings"), which is some blend of claims 2, 3, and 4 distributed across subsections. They have to do the bookkeeping themselves. I'd suggest either (a) adding a sentence at the start of each section that says which claim(s) it discharges, or (b) making the Plan of Attack mirror the section structure more directly. Option (a) is less work and probably better, since the conceptual framing is genuinely the more illuminating one.

The overall arc — simple settings build intuition, intuition transfers to the real setting, intuition yields an algorithm — is a good arc and it does land. The payoff (two-step alignment) feels earned. So the bones are good; it's the connective tissue that's thin.
## Where the terseness actually hurts
There's a difference between terseness that trusts the reader and terseness that hides a gap. Most of yours is the second kind, unfortunately, and it clusters around the claims you most want believed. Some specific spots:

**The conserved quantity claims are asserted, not shown.** In the scalar/scalar case you write that $\dot p = q, \dot q = p$ gives conserved quantity $p^2 - q^2$. Fine, that's one line of checking and a reader can do it. But then for Muon you jump from $\dot p = 1, \dot q = 1$ to "$p - q$ is a conserved quantity" — also fine — and then immediately to the Hamiltonian framing. The Hamiltonian claim is the one doing real conceptual work (it's claim 2, and it recurs), and you give it almost no room. You state $H = q - p$ and verify Hamilton's equations in one line, then invoke Liouville. This is the conceptual heart of your "Muon is qualitatively different" story and it gets two sentences. A reader who isn't already comfortable with Hamiltonian flows will not feel the force of it. I'd expand: say explicitly _why_ divergence-free matters here (volume preservation in phase space → no attraction to a point → the oscillation-not-convergence behavior you mention later is a _consequence_ of this, which you never connect). Right now Liouville appears, does its job, and the connection to the actual training behavior (no convergence, only oscillation) is left for the reader to draw — even though you _do_ draw it later in the figure discussion. Connect those two places explicitly.

**The vector-factor conserved quantity changes form without comment.** GD conserves $|\vp|^2 - |\vq|^2$ but Muon conserves $|\vp| - |\vq|$ (no squares). That's a meaningful and slightly surprising difference, and it's exactly the kind of thing a careful reader will pause on. You state both and move on. One sentence on why the orthogonalization removes the square would be worth a lot — it's a small "aha" that reinforces the whole "Muon controls magnitudes directly" theme.

**The "align to the bisector" claim is stated three times but never argued.** In the scalar/vector section you assert that the parameter vectors "align to the bisector of the initial vectors" regardless of learning rate regime. This is a nice geometric fact and it's load-bearing for the single-step alignment trick, but it appears as a bare assertion. Even a two-line geometric argument (the update to $\vp$ is along $\hat\vq$ and vice versa, symmetric, so the symmetric axis is preserved) would make it click. As written, the reader either already sees it or has to take it on faith, and the figure caption restates the claim without proving it.

**The decoupling argument moves fast at the key step.** Section 3.3 is the linchpin of claim 4 and it's good, but the crucial move — that the initial update rule is "remarkably similar" to inner-product factorization and that "the rows of $P$ and the rows of $Q$ are updated as if they were parameter vectors" — deserves to be made fully explicit rather than gestured at. Right now you derive $P^{(1)} \approx P^{(0)} + \frac{\eta}{\alpha}Q^{(0)}$ and then say it's like inner-product factorization. Show the parallel mechanically: write the per-row update and put it next to the inner-product update from 3.2 so the reader sees them line up. This is the bridge the whole paper walks across; widen it.

**The orthogonalization-at-initialization approximation is asserted with hand-waving about "niceties of high-dimensional space."** You use $\ort(\nabla_P\mc L) \approx -\frac{1}{\alpha}Q$ a couple of times. The step from $\nabla_P\mc L \approx -M^*Q$ to its orthogonalization being $\approx -\frac{1}{\alpha}Q$ uses that $Q$ has near-orthonormal rows scaled by $\alpha$, i.e. $\ort$ of something with singular values all near $\alpha$ divides by $\alpha$. That's true but it's quietly assuming $M^*Q$ inherits $Q$'s conditioning, which deserves a clause. This is the kind of place where one more sentence converts "trust me" into "of course."
## Smaller flow and correctness things
Also in the second-update equations (the $P^{(2)}, Q^{(2)}$ block), the prose right after refers to $\ort(M^*Q^{(1)})$ while the display equations use $\ort(M^*P^{(1)})$. Since $P^{(1)} = Q^{(1)}$ at that point they're equal, but the inconsistency reads as a slip — pick one and be consistent, or note explicitly "using $P^{(1)} = Q^{(1)}$."

Section 2 says "there are roughly two goals" and then "We delineate two different types of weight alignment" — so really three goals (two alignments plus value growth), and the three _phases_ in the Plan of Attack were alignment / growth / convergence. The counting ("two goals," "two types," "three phases") is a little dizzying across sections. Tighten the vocabulary so "goal," "type of alignment," and "phase" map cleanly and consistently.

The convergence/oscillation phase (third phase, claim 1) gets almost nothing in the body. Section 2's last paragraph mentions oscillation, and the scalar figure discussion mentions it, but there's no dedicated treatment despite it being one of your three phases. Either give it a short subsection or soften the "three phases" framing so the reader doesn't expect symmetric treatment and feel the gap.

The underparameterized subsection (4.1) ends a touch abruptly — "we can still spike the learning rate to quickly grow the singular values" — without saying what's lost relative to the clean two-step story. Is alignment now slow (gradient-flow regime) and only the _growth_ gets the spike? Say so; right now it reads as "the trick fails, but also kind of works," which is unsatisfying as a closer for the whole technical development.
## Direct answer to your two questions
On narrative flow: the macro-arc works and is satisfying. The two weaknesses are (1) the concept-vs-setting organizational mismatch I described, which makes the reader do mapping work, and (2) the lack of explicit forward/backward connective sentences — you frequently prove something in setting X and then _use_ it in setting Y without a sentence saying "we now import the result from 3.2." You do this transition well once (the start of 3.3 explicitly previews the decoupling) — that paragraph is a model for what the others should look like. Replicate that move at each setting boundary.

One last thing worth saying: the ideas are strong enough that the terseness currently undersells them. The single-step alignment result in particular is a clean, memorable, and (as far as I can tell) novel-feeling contribution, and right now it arrives almost casually. Give your best results a bit more ceremony.
